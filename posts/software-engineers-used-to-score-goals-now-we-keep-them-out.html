Software Engineers Used to Score Goals. Now We Keep Them Out.

July 5, 2026 · 2 min read

[An apology to my fellow software engineers for the football analogy (or rather soccer, if that's what you call the sport where you use your foot to pass the ball). Myself I'm not a huge football fan, but the analogy was there and the time is right]

Engineering culture is going through a tectonic shift: we used to be the strikers. We're becoming the goalkeepers.

For years, engineers were the ones leading the charge on product, solving the complex questions, being the ones announcing "we delivered this and that new features." With the emergence of the builder role, that's changing. The engineering role is starting to look a lot more like what DevEx and Production teams did in pre-AI orgs: making sure the org's builders can build a stable, scalable app. Not scoring the goals. Making sure nothing gets past us.

As engineering leaders, we have to catch up too — motivating a striker is easy (score, celebrate, repeat). However, motivating a goalkeeper is a different job: the wins are invisible, the failures are very visible, and success is measured by what didn't happen. Hiring changes too. The goalkeeper role calls for a different personality type and a different mentality — people wired for building trust, longevity and quiet success stories rather than the glory and fame. And the engineers we'll need next aren't the ones we optimized our interviews for over the last decade.

"The formulation of a problem is often more essential than its solution.", Einstein.

And for those of you saying "I know who Pelé is, but who is Buffon?" — well, that's where my analogy breaks (or maybe it doesn't. Football fans, please help). Here's the thing: for those of us who love problem solving, who love constantly finding new applications for the available technology, these are golden times. And the smarter the AI gets, the better it will get. Less writing React components and setting up the API between service A and service B, and more answering fundamental questions like how to build inherently secure apps, how to make genAI + builders produce code that's actually deliverable, and how the builder-coder (formerly known as product-R&D) org should be structured to tap into this potential growth.

Exciting times ahead!
